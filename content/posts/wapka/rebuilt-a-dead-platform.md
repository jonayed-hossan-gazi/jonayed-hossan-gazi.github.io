There was a platform called Wapka. It let anyone — on any device, in any country — build a website for free. It ran for years and served millions of people.

Then it shut down.

The community scattered. Hundreds of thousands of sites went offline. The forums went silent. Most people moved on.

I didn't.

---

## What Wapka actually was

To understand why I rebuilt it, you need to understand what it offered.

Wapka was a WAP site builder — Wireless Application Protocol, the mobile web before smartphones. You opened it on a Nokia feature phone, signed up, and instantly got a subdomain with a functioning database-driven website. No FTP. No server config. No terminal.

It came with pre-installed modules: a guestbook, a forum, a chatroom, a file manager. You could upload content, customize the theme, and embed dynamic features with simple tags. For millions of people — especially in regions where a laptop was a luxury — this was their first website. Their first line of code. Their first audience.

I was one of them.

---

## When it disappeared

One day it was gone. The domain stopped resolving. The forums went dead. Every site hosted on the platform — including mine — vanished.

I had learned HTML, CSS, and basic JavaScript on Wapka. It was where I first understood what a server was, what a database did, what it meant to build something that other people could access from anywhere in the world.

Losing it felt personal. But more than that — I understood something most people didn't: the need hadn't gone away. The product had.

There were still millions of people who wanted a free, simple way to build a website. They just didn't have Wapka anymore.

---

## The decision

I decided to rebuild it.

Not as a nostalgia project. Not as a clone. As a modern platform that preserved what made the original valuable — zero setup, instant publishing, server-side power without DevOps — and added everything the modern web demanded.

It took years. I'll tell that story in the next post.

But the core decision was this: I saw value where others saw a dead product. I believed the people who used Wapka deserved more than a 404 page and a memory. And I believed I could build it.

---

## Today

Over 100,000 sites run on Wapka. The code is open source. You can self-host with Docker. You can export your data and leave anytime. The platform has evolved through three eras — WAP tags, visual builder, Lua scripting — without breaking anything written in the first era.

What started as a teenager's stubborn idea has become a platform that serves creators, students, developers, and businesses across the world.

Not bad for something everyone else thought was dead.
